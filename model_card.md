# 🎧 Model Card: Music Recommender Simulation

**VibeSmash 8.0** — Applied AI Music Recommender with RAG and Confidence Scoring

---

## 1. Intended Use

VibeSmash 8.0 (refurbished) is a content-based music recommender designed for classroom exploration of applied AI concepts. It takes a listener's taste preferences (favorite genre, mood, target energy level, and acoustic preference) and returns the top 5 matching songs from a 50-song catalog, along with a confidence score and a natural language explanation generated by an LLM.

The system assumes the user can be described by a small set of attributes and that those attributes are stable for a given session. It makes no attempt to learn from listening history or adapt over time. This is an educational prototype, not a production recommendation engine.

---

## 2. How the Model Works

The system works in two stages. First, it scores every song in the catalog against the user's profile. A song earns points for matching the user's favorite genre, matching their preferred mood, and having an energy level close to their target. If the user likes acoustic music and a song is acoustic, it gets a small bonus. Songs are then ranked from highest to lowest score, and the top 5 are selected.

Second, those top 5 songs and the user's profile are handed off to a large language model (Groq's llama-3.3-70b-versatile). The model reads the song list and the user's preferences and writes a short paragraph explaining why those songs fit the listener. This is the RAG step: the scoring system retrieves the relevant songs, and the LLM generates a grounded explanation using only that retrieved context.

The confidence percentage shown next to each song is simply the raw score divided by the maximum possible score (4.5), converted to a percentage. A 89% confidence means the song matched nearly everything the user asked for.

---

## 3. Data

The catalog contains 50 songs spanning 7 genres: pop, lofi, rock, jazz, synthwave, ambient, and indie pop. Each song has 10 attributes: id, title, artist, genre, mood, energy, tempo, valence, danceability, and acousticness. The original dataset had 10 songs, which was expanded to 50 for this version to reduce genre imbalance.

Even at 50 songs, the catalog is still synthetic and small. Pop and lofi are better represented than ambient or synthwave. Moods like "sad" and "nostalgic" do not exist in the catalog at all, which means users who prefer those moods will always get mismatched recommendations. Real musical taste: involving lyrics, language, cultural context, and listening history is not captured here at all.

---

## 4. Strengths

The system works well for users whose preferences align with the most common genres in the catalog (pop, lofi, rock). When genre, mood, and energy all match, the confidence scores are high and the LLM explanations are accurate and specific. The conflicting profile test was a useful strength. The system correctly surfaced genre-matching songs even when mood had no match, and the LLM correctly identified the mismatch rather than pretending the results were perfect.

The confidence scoring adds real transparency. A user can see at a glance whether they got a 89% match or a 65% match, and understand why.

---

## 5. Limitations and Bias

Genre has the highest weight (+2.0 out of a maximum 4.5), which means genre almost always determines the top results regardless of how well mood or energy match. A pop song with the wrong mood will beat a perfectly-matched rock song just because of genre weight. This is a design choice but also a source of bias.

Users who prefer underrepresented genres (ambient, synthwave) will consistently get worse recommendations than pop or lofi fans, simply because fewer songs in the catalog match their genre. This is dataset bias, not algorithmic bias, but the effect on the user is the same. Listeners with niche or conflicting preferences (like sad mood + high energy) have no good match available and the system gives them the least-bad option without flagging that the results are weak.

The system also has no memory, no personalization, and no ability to learn. Every session starts fresh.

---

## 6. Evaluation

Four user profiles were tested: High-Energy Pop, Chill Lofi, Intense Rock, and Conflicting (sad + high energy). For the first three, the system returned results that matched intuition. The right genre and mood dominated the top 5, and confidence scores were consistently 88-89%. For the conflicting profile, no song matched both preferences, and confidence dropped to 65-67%, which correctly reflected the weaker match.

Five automated tests were written and all pass: score sorting, explanation output, genre boost behavior, k-limit enforcement, and acoustic preference scoring. The most surprising result was that the LLM explanation for the conflicting profile was the most useful output in the entire project. It identified the mood gap explicitly rather than generating a falsely positive explanation.

---

## 7. Future Work

The most impactful improvement would be adding more songs per genre, especially for underrepresented genres, and adding moods that are currently missing from the catalog (sad, melancholy, nostalgic). A secondary improvement would be adjusting the genre weight so that mood and energy can compete more fairly when genre is absent.

Longer term, adding user listening history and collaborative filtering (recommending based on what similar users liked) would make the system meaningfully more useful. The LLM explanation could also be improved by giving it access to the full scoring breakdown, not just the song list, so it can reference specific scores in its explanation.

## 8. Personal Reflection

This project made the mechanics of recommendation systems feel real in a way that reading about them did not. The conflicting profile was the most instructive moment. It showed that a system can produce confident-looking, neatly formatted output while completely failing the user's actual request. That gap between surface appearance and actual quality is something I will think about whenever I interact with a recommendation system going forward.

Working with the Groq LLM also changed how I think about RAG. The LLM explanation was only as good as what the retriever gave it. When the retriever returned a good match, the explanation was sharp and specific. When the retriever returned a weak match (the conflicting profile), the LLM still produced readable output, but it correctly flagged the problem rather than hiding it. That behavior: an AI that admits a partial failure. It honestly felt more trustworthy than one that would have generated a confident but misleading explanation.

**AI collaboration note:** One instance where AI assistance was genuinely helpful was in debugging the Groq model name when `llama3-8b-8192` was decommissioned, the suggestion to switch to `llama-3.3-70b-versatile` resolved the issue immediately. One instance where AI assistance was flawed was the repeated suggestion to try different Gemini API keys to resolve quota errors. The actual problem was account-level quota exhaustion, not a key issue, and the correct fix was switching providers entirely. Sure, it may not have been ideal, but it was a fix that I thought was best given the situation. After following the AI suggestion, I was able to save a ton of time in the long run.
